The £10,000 asylum charge is close to uncollectable. The right to work is worth many times more.

A technical companion to “The UK’s £10,000 Asylum Seeker Payment Must Be Paired With The Right To Work” — Helen Dempster, Lee Crawfurd & Ian Mitchell

Order-of-magnitude estimates. Every figure below independently re-derived. July 2026.

The government’s new Immigration & Asylum Bill lets the Home Office recover about £10,000 from asylum seekers who received support. Recovered student-loan-style from people the state forbids from working, that charge claws back only about a sixth of its face value in present value over a 40-year working life — and only from the ~quarter of refugees who reach and hold full-time earnings above the threshold. Most never do. Meanwhile the 12-month work ban it sits alongside costs the Exchequer several times as much, and far sooner, in lost tax and avoidable support. And the charge is parasitic on the right to work: every pound repaid comes from someone earning above the threshold, which the ban prevents. “Make them pay” is impossible without “let them work.”

Start with one person

Before the aggregates, follow a single asylum seeker who finds work — many never do, which is itself part of the story. The chart below builds up in three steps: today’s status quo (earnings and the tax they pay); then the £10,000 charge laid on top; then the same person with the right to work at three months. The ban doesn’t just delay work — it lowers employment for years afterwards, so an earlier start lifts the whole path (the “scarring” you see appear in the third step).

Figure 1. One working asylum seeker over 20 years (many never work at all). Press the step buttons, or let it play. Tax = income tax + employee NI; charge repayment = 9% above £25,000, capped at £10,000; the third step reflects a 3-month start and reduced scarring.

Three things jump out. The charge changes nothing about earnings — panels A and B are the same lines; bolting a debt onto someone doesn’t help them earn. Even from a worker it barely collects — £3.3k of £10k after twenty years. And the right to work pays the Exchequer far more through tax than the charge ever recovers — about £45k more over the period, more than four times the whole charge — while being the only scenario in which the charge is cleared.

Now scale it across a cohort

Multiply that logic across the ~85,000 adults who claim asylum each year. Three fiscal channels are in play. Channel A is the direct recovery of the £10,000 charge. Channel B is the support cost avoided when someone works while their claim is pending — which accrues whether or not the claim later succeeds. Channel C is the avoided scarring from the third step, now summed across everyone. On a like-for-like present-value basis the charge is several times smaller than the right to work — and, unlike it, collected over decades rather than up front.

Channel A — why the charge collects so little

The charge is recovered as 9% of income above a £25,000 threshold (the student-loan Plan 5 benchmark). Two facts sink it. First, refugee earnings are low: the Home Office’s own RIO data — measured only among those in work — shows just 6% of employed refugees earn ≥£20,000 in year one. Second, most refugees are not in work at all (employment is 24% in year one, ~48% by year eight). Combine the two and the share of all refugees above the £25,000 threshold runs from about 1% in year one to about 13% by year eight. Earnings grow — but even at the eight-year peak roughly seven in eight are still below the threshold, and repayment comes only from the minority who climb above it over a full career.

Figure 3. Share of all working-age refugees, year 1 → year 8 (RIO employment rates × RIO earnings among the employed). Earnings grow — the not-working share falls, the repaying share rises — but even by year 8, ~87% earn below the £25,000 threshold and repay nothing.

Recovered like an income-contingent student loan — 9% of earnings above the threshold, capped at £10,000, until written off at 40 years — and tracked on individual earner types in present value (the two choices that matter: only career progression crosses a wage-indexed threshold, and repayment is back-loaded into years 15–40), it recovers:

So over a working life the charge recovers about a sixth of face value in present value — not the ~5% a ten-year window suggests. In student-loan terms that is a ~84% RAB charge, far worse than the ~50% on graduate loans. It comes only from the ~22% who reach and hold full-time earnings above £25,000 (per working refugee, lifetime recovery is ~£5,600 — about 56% of the charge; the population figure is low because ~half never work). At a ~60% grant rate the aggregate is ~£82m in present value per cohort (~£48–109m across assumptions), collected over decades. The whole calculation is a runnable script — model.py.

The load-bearing assumptions are all policy-unspecified — the Bill sets neither a threshold nor a rate — so the model borrows the student-loan analogy the government itself invited. The biggest lever is how many ever earn above £25,000 (~a quarter, central); the next is the threshold, set here at Plan-5’s £25,000 ≈ full-time minimum wage, so the median full-time refugee worker repays nothing. Lower it and recovery rises — but you are then collecting from refugees on poverty wages, which contradicts the policy’s own “sufficient funds” premise.

Channels B & C — what the right to work is worth

Channel B — support saved. Letting one person work six months sooner offsets accommodation and subsistence plus a little tax — about £11,000 in that single transition. Crucially this accrues whether or not the claim later succeeds; housing someone in a hotel costs the same regardless. Across a cohort that is on the order of £140–234m.

Channel C — scarring avoided. Two independent causal studies agree the ban’s main damage is permanent: Fasani, Frattini & Minale (2020) find a ban cuts refugee employment by 15% for up to a decade; Hainmueller, Hangartner & Lawrence (2016) find each extra year of enforced limbo costs 4–5 percentage points of employment. Both imply a 12→6-month cut lifts employment by ~3 points, sustained for years — worth about £122m per cohort (~£108m in present value). On its own that is comparable to the charge’s entire lifetime recovery (~£82m PV); together with Channel B — and front-loaded, not spread over decades — the right to work is worth several times the charge.

A tougher asylum regime makes the charge less collectable, not more. Only granted-and-remaining refugees ever repay, so cutting the grant rate shrinks the charge’s base — while the biggest right-to-work benefit (not housing people who could support themselves) pays off even for applicants later refused.

“But won’t a job make them harder to remove?”

This is the objection most likely to be raised — and the evidence points the other way: legal work probably makes removal easier, not harder. Three reasons.

Legal work makes people more visible, not less. A job on PAYE means a known employer, a known address, a National Insurance number and a tax record. The alternative to legal work is not idleness but informal work — invisible to the Home Office. A work ban pushes people into the shadow economy, which is exactly where they are hardest to trace.

The state’s grip runs through reporting, not the hotel. People awaiting decisions are not detained — they are free to come and go. Contact is kept through monthly digital reporting, which continues whether or not someone works, and work permission is already refused to anyone who has absconded — so a legal job (fixed employer, fixed address) can be a condition of compliance, not a route to disappearing. The status quo is already leaky: ~50,000 people are logged as absconders under a system whose own data the Home Office calls “incomplete, inconsistent or simply unavailable” — with no right to work.

The legal worry is weak. The strongest version — that employment builds an Article 8 private-life defence against removal — runs into the fact that UK courts heavily discount private life built during “precarious status.” What strengthens such claims is length of residence, which the backlog produces regardless of whether anyone works.

And it only bites for a minority. At a ~60% grant rate, most people are staying anyway — so working sooner just means integrating sooner. The concern applies only to the ~40% eventually refused, of whom most are not removed in any case. There is no direct evidence that employment makes removal harder; it sits in the same unevidenced category as the “pull factor” claim the Migration Advisory Committee has twice asked the government to substantiate, with nothing produced.

The robust conclusion across all of these: the charge recovers well under 10% of face value, and the right to work is worth several times more. The soft numbers are the inputs (earnings shares, employment response, grant rate), not the arithmetic.

Bottom line

  1. The charge, alone, is close to fiscally pointless — ~3% of what it notionally bills, ~£2.4m/yr per cohort.
  2. “Make them pay” is impossible without “let them work.” You cannot recover £10,000 from someone earning nothing, and the state is the reason they earn nothing.
  3. The fiscal prize is the work ban, not the charge — in present value the right to work is worth several times what the charge recovers, and front-loaded rather than collected over decades; most of that prize is permanent scarring avoided, not a six-month delay.
  4. The evidence points shorter than six months — the harm is front-loaded, so a 6-month ban is a compromise, not a cure.
  5. Reciprocity is the frame, and it’s the model’s result: if we expect asylum seekers to repay, we have to let them earn.

Method & sources

Deterministic expected-value model over the Home Office RIO earnings distribution; nominal, undiscounted 10- and 20-year sums. Every quantitative claim was independently re-derived by separate agents blind to the stated answers; two errors were caught and fixed in the process (an aggregate arithmetic slip, and treating employment-conditional earnings as population shares). Residual uncertainty sits in the inputs — the conditional earnings shares, the employment response to lifting the ban, and the ~60% grant rate — not in the sums.
