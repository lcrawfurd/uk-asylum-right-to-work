# The £10,000 asylum charge will collect almost nothing. The right to work would collect far more.

*Helen Dempster, Lee Crawfurd & Ian Mitchell*

The government's new Immigration & Asylum Bill would recover about £10,000 from asylum seekers who received support. It sounds like a way to defray costs. But on the government's own data, the charge would collect almost nothing — and only from the minority of refugees who go on to well-paid, full-time work. The right to work, by contrast, would raise several times more, and far sooner.

How much might the government actually recover, and how would that change if asylum seekers could work? The government hasn't published full details of the scheme, but says payment is conditional, "provided they have access to sufficient funds." So we assume a repayment scheme like student loans: 9 percent of earnings above a threshold of £25,000. The obvious challenge is that very few asylum seekers earn above this.

> **What we assume — and how it moves the answer**
>
> - **Repayment works like a student loan: 9% of earnings above £25,000, capped at £10,000.** The scheme isn't specified, so we borrow the [student-loan Plan 5](https://www.gov.uk/repaying-your-student-loan/what-you-pay) terms. *The threshold is the biggest lever: set it lower and the charge collects more — but from refugees on poverty wages.*
> - **Earnings follow the Home Office's own refugee data** ([RIO](https://www.gov.uk/government/statistics/immigration-system-statistics-year-ending-december-2024)). Half never sustain full-time work; those who do earn a median of £23,000 — *below the threshold, so the median worker repays nothing.*
> - **Only about 60% are eventually granted asylum**, and only they can ever repay (the rest are removed, or barred from working legally). *A tougher asylum regime collects less, not more.*
> - **We count repayment over a full 40-year working life, in today's money.** *Over ten years it looks like ~5%; over a career it reaches ~16% in present value — but back-loaded, and paid only by the minority who do well.*

Below we follow a typical earnings pathway for a refugee and the tax they contribute over 20 years. In the second step we add the £10,000 charge. In the third step we keep the charge but let the refugee work after three months, which shifts both immediate and later earnings upward.

## For individual asylum seekers

Most asylum seekers don't earn enough to pay the charge. Even a refugee who does relatively well — reaching £31,000 — repays just **£3,300** over 20 years. The typical full-time worker, on £23,000, repays **nothing at all**: that is below the £25,000 threshold. Only the top quarter of earners ever clear the full £10,000.

The right to work changes the picture. In the third step, the same refugee contributes about **four times more in tax (£45,000)** than the entire charge would over the same period — and starts repaying the charge around **five years sooner**.

*Figure 1. Over 20 years, the charge collects little from one worker — the right to work collects far more.*

## Across the whole asylum-seeker population

Around 80,000 people claim asylum in the UK every year. In our model, only about **1 percent** earn above the £25,000 threshold in year one, rising to about **13 percent** by year eight. Spread over a full working life and discounted to today's money, the charge recovers only about **a sixth of its face value — roughly £77 million per cohort**. Even someone earning £30,000 — already in the top tenth of refugees — would repay only about £450 a year.

Instead, the Home Office could let asylum seekers start working after three months, while their claim is pending. This would offset the accommodation and subsistence costs they incur, while also bringing in tax — about **£11,000** per person who works sooner. These contributions accrue regardless of whether the asylum claim ultimately succeeds. Across the entire cohort, this would be worth around **£130–220 million**.

Allowing people to work earlier also has dynamic benefits. Work bans don't just delay employment — they suppress it for years. [Fasani et al. (2020)](https://docs.iza.org/dp13149.pdf) find a ban cuts refugee employment by 15 percent for up to a decade; [Hainmueller et al. (2016)](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.1600432) find every extra year of enforced idleness costs 4–5 percentage points. Both imply that cutting the ban from twelve to six months would lift employment by about **three percentage points — worth around £115 million per cohort**.

The conclusion is clear. The charge recovers only a fraction of its value, collected over decades, while the right to work is worth **several times more** and arrives up front.

## What about hotel costs?

Hotel costs for asylum seekers are running at around [£3 billion a year](https://www.nao.org.uk/reports/investigation-into-asylum-accommodation/). Much of that would be unnecessary if asylum seekers could work. Home Office figures show about a quarter of refugees are in work within a year of being granted status, rising to nearly half after two years. Some of that work is part-time, and some applicants are in families — but if even **10 to 25 percent** of those who could work preferred their own accommodation (where they could cook, for example) and so came off support entirely, that alone would save **£300–750 million a year**. {>>This range = 10–25% of the £3bn hotel bill, on the assumption that a self-supporting worker leaves hotel accommodation altogether. The 10–25% is our judgement, not a measured figure — soften or firm as you prefer.<<}

## A double inefficiency is wasting public money

The way the UK houses asylum seekers suffers from two flaws. First, it puts applicants in hotels at **£144.98 a night — over six times the £23.25** that [dispersal accommodation costs](https://www.nao.org.uk/reports/investigation-into-asylum-accommodation/). Second, it bars them from working while their claim proceeds.

Letting asylum seekers work and pay tax would save **hundreds of millions a year — and, across successive cohorts, billions** — for the UK taxpayer. It would also disperse the highly visible, politically damaging clusters of applicants left with nothing to do but stand around outside town-centre hotels.

Expecting asylum seekers to pay for their own accommodation where they can — saving money and returning hotels to their proper use — would surely be a better way forward than a tokenistic attempt to reclaim some costs 20 years down the line.
